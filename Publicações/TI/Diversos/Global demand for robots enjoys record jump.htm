News November 28, 2003
Global demand for robots enjoys record jump
 
Demand for robots jumped by a best-ever 26% in the first half of 2003, signalling a recovery in the world's mechanical workforce.

The World Robotics 2003 survey said the robot business in Japan - still the largest in the world - was slow, but it was improving in Europe and the US.

"These figures indicate that a strong recovery is in sight," the report, by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE) and International Federation of Robotics (IFR), said.

The survey found robots were branching beyond carmaking into new fields such as entertainment and household chores.

IFR managing director Thilo Brodtmann said small and medium-sized businesses were starting to benefit from the technology.

And he dismissed fears that a growth in mechanical staff takes jobs away from living and breathing bodies.

"Robots can create jobs as they offer more of a reason to invest in the manufacturing industry," he said.

"People are needed to look after them, also robots do a lot of the hard work, such as heavy manual tasks, offering chances for humans to do better jobs."

Half-yearly results suggested worldwide robot orders for the whole of 2003 would rise above 80,000 units from 68,600 last year, the survey said.

But the estimate was far short of a record high of almost 99,000 robots ordered in 2000 as the industry continued to recover from the recent global economic slowdown.

The report predicted that the market for industrial robots would grow at an annual rate of 7.4% to just over 91,000 units in 2006.

Meanwhile, it found demand for mechanical labour in Europe and the US was fast catching up with Japan.

The ratio of multipurpose industrial robots in the EU, compared with all robots in Japan, rose to 67% in 2002 from 23% in 1990.

In the US the figure increased to 30% from 12%.

"The strong auto industry in Europe is part of the explanation (for the increase)," Brodtmann said.

"Also Japan's business climate has decreased over the past ten years."

Despite this trend, Japan's robot industry would remain larger than other regions for the immediate future.

The report estimated there would be some 875,000 robots in use in 2005, of which 333,000 would be in Japan, 303,000 in the European Union and 135,000 in North America.
